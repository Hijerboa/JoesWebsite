Computer Buying Guide



Reading the Computer Catalog

Buying a computer can be a very confusing process, especially if you aren't tech savy, and don't know what all the terms involved with the purchasing of a computer mean. Obviously more expensive is better, but what about if you're looking at two similarly priced computers? Is it worth spending the extra $25? Thats where this guide can help you decide.

There are two key questions you should ask yourself when you buy a new computer: 1.) What are you planning on using your computer for? and 2.) How much are you looking to spend?

With these two questions in mind, it will be fairly easy to determine the best possible computer for you.


The Processor

When you hear of the "brain" of the computer, the processor is what is being referred to. The processor of the computer is argueably the most vital part of any computer, as it is what actually performs all the operations necessary for your computer to run

When looking at a processor, there are three key things to be looking at: what processor it actually is, what the clock speed is, and how many cores it has.

Processor Name

When looking at the name of the processor, the most important thing to look at is the brand. There are only two brands used in modern Windows computers: Intel and AMD. Generally, Intel's processors perform slightly better than AMD processors in general every day tasks, but when mulitasking heavily, such as having many windows open at once, AMD processors tend to perform as well as, and sometimes even slightly better than Intel processors.

Of course, this is a general guideline, and will not always be true: in the past two years or so, the difference has become much less stark, and there is little difference for the average consumer between and AMD processor and an Intel processor.

Clock Speed

A processors clock speed (or just speed) is measured in hertz. One hertz means that the processor can perform one operation per second. Of course, any computer performing only one operation, or even a hundred operations per second, would be incredibly slow. As such, most processors run at a speed of greater than 1 Gigahertz (gHz), meaning greater than a billion operations per second. As such, the faster the processor is in gHz, the faster the computer will be able to perform tasks.

Core Count

A core of a processor is essentially a mini processor within the processor. Each core can do operations independently of the other. Thus, a quad-core processor with four cores can be doing four operations at once. Thus, a multi-core processor will perfrom significanlty faster than a single core processor. But will a dual core processor always be twice as fast as a single processor of the same speed? Unfortuantly not. Due to the fact that not all workloads can take advantage of multicore funtionatliy. Even those that are able to don't perform as efficiently as the theoretically can: the operating system has to expend some peformance delgating tasks between the cores, which uses a surprising amount of power. Thus, while more cores are definatly better than less cores, it is not always as fast as it can possibly be.
